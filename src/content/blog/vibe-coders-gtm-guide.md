You've built something. Maybe with Cursor, maybe with Lovable, maybe with Claude Code. It works. It looks decent. You're proud of it.

Now what?

This is the part where most technical founders get stuck. Building is the fun bit. Selling feels weird, uncomfortable, and vaguely beneath you. I get it. But if nobody uses what you built, what was the point?

I've spent 15+ years doing go-to-market for a living, and I now work almost exclusively with founders who can build but can't sell. Here's the guide I wish someone had given them earlier.

## First: Accept That Marketing Is Not a Dirty Word

I see this all the time. A founder builds something genuinely useful, puts it on GitHub or Product Hunt, gets a few stars or upvotes, and then... nothing. They assume if the product is good enough, people will find it.

They won't. Sorry. The internet is too big and too noisy. Marketing isn't about manipulation — it's about making sure the people who need your thing know it exists. That's it.

Once you reframe marketing as "helping the right people find something useful," it stops feeling gross.

## Step 1: Answer Three Questions

Before you do anything else, get clear on these:

### What problem do you solve?

Not "what does your product do" — what problem does it solve? There's a difference.

"We built a real-time collaborative markdown editor" is a feature description.
"Technical teams waste hours getting docs out of sync across tools" is a problem.

Your problem statement should make your target customer nod and say "yes, that's annoying."

### Who has this problem most acutely?

Not "everyone." Not "developers." Not "small businesses." Who specifically?

"DevOps engineers at companies with 10-50 employees who use Notion but hate it for technical documentation."

The more specific, the better. A narrow audience you deeply understand beats a broad audience you're guessing about.

### Why should they care about your solution specifically?

There are probably other ways to solve this problem. Why yours? Maybe it's faster. Maybe it's cheaper. Maybe it integrates with something they already use. Maybe you just built it better for their specific workflow.

This isn't about being objectively "the best." It's about being the best fit for your specific audience.

## Step 2: Find Your First Users

You don't need a marketing budget. You need to go where your people already are.

### GitHub

If your target users are technical, GitHub is goldmine. Search for repos in adjacent spaces. Look at who's starring similar projects. Check the issues on competing tools — those frustrated users might be your first fans.

I literally mine GitHub for leads using AI agents. You don't need to go that far, but browsing GitHub for potential users is something every technical founder should do.

### Communities

Reddit, Discord servers, Slack groups, indie hacker forums. Where do your target users hang out and talk about the problem you solve?

Don't join and immediately promote your product. That's how you get banned. Join, contribute genuinely for a week or two, and then mention your project when it's naturally relevant. "I was having this exact problem, so I built something to solve it" goes over much better than "Check out my product!"

### Twitter/X

Tech Twitter is noisy but powerful. The "build in public" approach works if you're genuine about it. Share what you're building, what's hard, what you're learning. People follow the journey, and some of them become users.

### Product Hunt

Good for a launch spike, not for sustained growth. Plan your launch day carefully — have people ready to upvote and comment. But don't pin all your hopes on it. The traffic fades fast.

## Step 3: Outreach That Doesn't Feel Like Spam

Cold outreach has a terrible reputation because most of it is terrible. But done right, it's the most effective way to get your first 10-20 customers.

Here's the framework:

**Be specific.** Reference something real about the person or their company. "I saw your project [name] on GitHub and noticed you're handling [specific thing] manually" is miles better than "I noticed your innovative company."

**Lead with value, not a pitch.** Share an insight, a resource, or a genuine observation before asking for anything. "I tested your app in French and found three UX issues — happy to share the details" gives before it takes.

**Keep it short.** Under 100 words. Seriously. Nobody reads long cold emails. Get to the point.

**Make the ask small.** Don't ask for a 30-minute call. Ask if they'd be open to a quick chat. Or even better, ask a question they can answer with a short reply. Get the conversation started.

**Follow up once, then stop.** One follow-up is fine. Two is pushing it. Three is spam. If they don't reply after a follow-up, move on.

## Step 4: The Minimum Viable GTM Stack

You don't need a $500/month marketing tech stack. Here's what actually matters at early stage:

**A landing page that converts.** One page. Clear headline, clear problem statement, clear CTA. Not "request a demo" — nobody wants a demo. "Try it free" or "See how it works."

**An email list.** Even a simple one. Buttondown, ConvertKit, even a Google Form. When someone shows interest but isn't ready to use your product yet, capture their email. You'll want to reach them later.

**Analytics.** At minimum, know how many people visit your site, where they come from, and what they do. Plausible or PostHog, not Google Analytics (your technical users will judge you for the latter).

**A way to talk to users.** Discord, Slack, email — something where early users can tell you what they need. This is your most valuable channel and it costs nothing.

That's it. You don't need a CRM yet. You don't need marketing automation. You don't need a content calendar. Those come later, when you have enough users to justify the complexity.

## Step 5: When to Hire vs When to Automate

This is where I see founders waste money. They hire a marketing person before they have product-market fit. That person has no idea what to do because the founder hasn't figured out the positioning yet. Everyone's frustrated.

**Automate first:**
- Lead discovery (GitHub mining, community monitoring)
- Email sequences (Smartlead, Lemlist, or similar)
- Analytics and reporting
- Social posting (if you batch-create content)

**Hire when:**
- You have a repeatable process that works but you can't keep up with the volume
- You need someone to manage relationships with larger accounts
- You're ready to expand into channels you don't understand (paid ads, partnerships, enterprise sales)

The general rule: if you can describe exactly what the person should do, you can probably automate it first. If you need someone to figure out what to do, that's when you hire.

## The Mindset Shift

The biggest barrier for technical founders isn't skill or resources — it's the belief that building a great product is the hard part and selling it should be easy.

It's actually the opposite. Building is (increasingly) the easy part, especially with AI tools. The hard part is finding people who care, convincing them to try it, and keeping them around.

That's not a bad thing. It just means you need to spend as much time understanding your users as you spend writing code. Talk to them. Watch them use your product. Ask them what's frustrating. The answers will inform everything — your positioning, your outreach, your roadmap.

## Start Today

Don't read this and add "figure out GTM" to your backlog. Do one thing today:

- Write down the problem you solve in one sentence
- Find three communities where your target users hang out
- Send one message to someone who might care about what you built

That's a start. Everything else follows from there.
