People ask me what I do and I never have a clean answer. "I run four companies from Latvia" gets a look — usually somewhere between impressed and concerned.

The honest version: I'm an English guy who moved to Latvia, married a Latvian woman, and somehow ended up running a localisation company, an energy brokerage, a heritage tech project, and a GTM tools business. All from a laptop, mostly from my flat in Riga, with AI doing a lot of the heavy lifting.

Here's what that actually looks like day to day.

## The Companies

### Globalize
This is the main one. Globalize is a localisation company — we help businesses translate and adapt their products for international markets. Not just word-for-word translation, but proper localisation: cultural adaptation, UI/UX for different languages, the whole thing.

I'm the CEO. We have a team, real clients, proper revenue. This is the company that pays the bills.

### Edge Energy
An energy brokerage. We help UK businesses get better energy deals. I built a deal room portal (AI-powered, naturally) that replaced the spreadsheet chaos that most brokerages run on. It's a proper business with brokers, customers, and pipeline.

### Gotomar
A GTM strategy tool for technical founders. You answer questions about your product and market, and it generates a personalised go-to-market playbook. Built the whole thing with Claude Code. It's live, it has users, and it's growing.

### Saknes
The passion project. A heritage mapping platform for the Latvian diaspora. Interactive map based on the 1935 topographic survey, where people can pin family properties, ancestors, and heritage items. This one's personal — my wife's family history inspired it.

## The Daily Workflow

I don't have a rigid schedule, but there's a rhythm.

**Morning (8-9am): Check the machines.**
First thing I do is open Discord. My AI agents post overnight summaries there — new leads found, campaign performance, any errors or anomalies. This is my dashboard. Takes 10 minutes to know where everything stands.

Then I check email and Notion. Notion is where I track tasks across all four companies. Each company has a board. I scan for anything urgent.

**Mid-morning (9am-12pm): The deep work block.**
This is when I build things. Claude Code sessions, usually. Might be adding a feature to Gotomar, fixing a bug in the deal room portal, or working on Saknes. No meetings in this block if I can help it.

**Afternoon (1-3pm): Communication and management.**
Calls with the Globalize team. Client meetings. Responding to emails that need actual thought. This is also when I review what the AI agents have done and make any strategic decisions — approve a new campaign, adjust targeting, review lead quality.

**Late afternoon (3-5pm): Admin and planning.**
The boring but necessary stuff. Invoicing, contracts, planning next week's priorities. Also when I write — blog posts, documentation, content for the companies.

**Evening: Nothing.**
I stop. This is important. I'll get to why.

## How AI Agents Handle the Repetitive Work

The reason four companies is even remotely possible is that I've automated the repetitive parts.

**Lead generation** runs on autopilot. The Dwarf agent mines GitHub daily, Qualy scores the leads, and Smartlead sends the emails. I set up campaigns once and they run indefinitely.

**Client communication** at Edge Energy is mostly automated through the deal room portal. Stage transitions trigger emails, reminders fire automatically, and HubSpot stays updated without manual entry.

**Reporting** is handled by Rex, who compiles performance data across campaigns and posts daily summaries. I read a Discord message instead of logging into five different dashboards.

**Follow-ups** are managed by Angie and Eddy. When leads go cold or show engagement signals, appropriate outreach happens without me writing individual emails.

None of this is perfect. I still manually review things, make strategic calls, and handle anything that requires genuine human relationship-building. But the volume work — the stuff that would normally need 2-3 people — runs without me.

## The Stack That Makes It Possible

Here's what I actually use every day:

- **Claude Code** — for building everything. Features, fixes, new tools, scripts. This is my most important tool by far.
- **Discord** — command centre. Agent notifications, team chat for Globalize, personal task reminders.
- **Notion** — project management across all companies. Boards, docs, meeting notes.
- **Smartlead** — outbound email campaigns. Handles sending, warmup, and reply management.
- **HubSpot** — CRM for Globalize and Edge Energy. Pipeline management, deal tracking.
- **Vercel** — where most of my apps are deployed. Serverless, scales automatically.
- **Supabase** — database and auth for everything I build. Brilliant product.
- **GitHub** — code repos and GitHub Actions for scheduled agent runs.

Total monthly cost for all of this: less than you'd think. Most of these tools have generous free tiers or affordable starter plans. The most expensive line item is Smartlead, and that's because email deliverability tooling is genuinely worth paying for.

## The Lifestyle (Honestly)

I need to address the elephant in the room: this sounds like hustle culture. "I run four companies!" sounds like the kind of thing someone says while posting at 3am with a caption about grinding.

That's not what this is.

I live in Riga. It's a beautiful, affordable, quiet city. My wife and I have a nice life. I work normal-ish hours. I eat dinner at home. I don't work weekends unless something is genuinely on fire (which is rare because the agents handle most emergencies).

The reason I can manage four things isn't that I work harder than everyone else. It's that I've built leverage. AI agents handle the volume. Automation handles the routine. I handle the strategy and relationships.

Some weeks are heavier than others. When Globalize has a big client onboarding, that takes priority and the other companies run on autopilot for a few days. When I'm deep in a Saknes build session, Globalize is fine because the team is good.

It's not about doing four things simultaneously. It's about doing the right thing for each company at the right time, and trusting the systems to handle the rest.

## What Each Company Needs from Me

This is the bit people find surprising. Each company needs different things from me, and none of them need everything.

**Globalize** needs strategic leadership, client relationships, and team management. I don't do the actual localisation work — we have linguists and project managers for that.

**Edge Energy** needs the deal room portal to work and the pipeline to move. The brokers handle the customer relationships. I built the tools and occasionally handle big accounts.

**Gotomar** needs product development. It's early stage, so I'm building features and talking to users. This is mostly Claude Code sessions and customer conversations.

**Saknes** needs focused building time. It's still in development, so it's a project more than a business right now. I give it blocks of time when I can.

## What's Genuinely Hard

It's not all smooth. Here's what I find difficult:

**Context switching.** Going from an energy brokerage problem to a heritage mapping feature to a GTM strategy discussion is mentally exhausting. I try to batch by company — mornings for building (Gotomar/Saknes), afternoons for managing (Globalize/Edge) — but it doesn't always work.

**Saying no.** Every company has opportunities. Globalize could expand into new markets. Edge could add new services. Gotomar could add features. Saknes could do events. I can't do all of them. Saying no to good ideas is the hardest part of running multiple things.

**Isolation.** Working from a laptop in Latvia means I don't have a co-working space full of other founders to bounce ideas off. Discord and video calls help, but it's not the same as being in a room with people. I miss that sometimes.

**Explaining it.** "What do you do?" at a dinner party is genuinely awkward. I usually just say "I run a translation company" and leave it at that.

## Would I Recommend It?

Running four companies? Honestly, no. Not for most people. It works for me because of specific circumstances: the AI leverage, a supportive partner, companies at different stages that need different things, and the low cost of living in Latvia that takes financial pressure off.

What I would recommend: using AI tools to build more than you think you can. Whether that's one company with better tooling, or a side project alongside your main job, or automating the boring parts of your work so you can focus on what matters.

The leverage is real. The laptop-from-anywhere thing is real. You just need to be honest about what you're good at, what you should automate, and when to stop working for the day.
